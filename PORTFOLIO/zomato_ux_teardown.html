01 · The Problem

The Price Paradox

A friend once said he'd rather order from Swiggy — even if Zomato was cheaper. That one sentence is pretty much the whole problem.

Zomato genuinely has a price advantage. It runs deeper discounts, more aggressive offers, and more frequent deals. But none of that lands if the UI itself is doing the opposite of what the discounts are trying to do. Price advantage only matters when people trust you enough to act on it.

Swiggy, by contrast, lets the food do the talking. The interface is quieter. The photography sits bigger. The whole thing just feels premium — and premium is a feeling, not a number.

Zomato's UI Signal
"We need your order. Please."
Swiggy's UI Signal
"The food speaks for itself."
02 · Home Screen Comparison

First Impressions

Here's the thing — the perception problem doesn't actually live on the home screen. And this is where most people get the critique wrong. Zomato's home screen is genuinely solid.

Zomato Home Swiggy Home
Background White, clean, nothing in the way Dark purple — immediately heavy
Food Cards Large, minimal overlay — the food shows Three competing banners fighting for space
Colour Use Restrained, tasteful Orange discount badges sitting on photos
Navigation Standard, nothing weird A Bollywood mascot tab in the bottom nav
Overall Actually the stronger home ✔ Busier, more cluttered

The real damage happens further in — on the restaurant page, after the user has clicked through and is actively deciding what to order.

03 · Restaurant Page

Where Zomato Loses the Order

Five issues pile up on one screen. The two critical ones aren't just friction — they actively block the user from completing an order.

Before — Original App

What we're looking at here: the grey ADD button, the yellow warning sitting mid-screen, and that red login bar pinned to the bottom right when the user is ready to add something.

Each element on its own might be defensible. Together, they compound into something that reads as chaotic and low-confidence.

Issue 01 Critical
Login Gate Blocking Checkout

Users can browse the menu just fine — but the moment they tap ADD, a red "Login to Continue" bar fires. Right at the peak of intent. The decision to order has already been made; this is the worst possible moment to introduce friction. Most people don't abandon browsing. They abandon checkout.

Issue 02 Critical
The ADD Button Looks Like a Form Field

Grey. The colour of disabled states. The ADD button, paired with "customisable" printed beneath it in small text, reads like a spreadsheet data entry field. There's no visual signal that says "tap this to buy something." A button that doesn't feel like a button doesn't get tapped.

Issue 03 Major
A Warning Banner Where the Menu Should Be

"Delivery is managed by the restaurant" sits in a prominent yellow banner dead in the middle of the screen. This is a legal disclosure. It belongs at the bottom, in footnote text. Putting it centre-stage — right when someone is deciding whether to order — plants a seed of doubt at the worst possible moment.

Issue 04 Major
No Visual Hierarchy — Everything Shouts at Once

The restaurant name, delivery info, offers, badges, filter chips, and menu items all compete at the same visual weight. The user's eye has nowhere to land first. When everything is important, nothing is. Good hierarchy gives users a path; this gives them noise.

Issue 05 Minor
The Food Is an Afterthought

Food delivery is an emotional purchase. The photography should be the first thing you see and the thing that makes you hungry. Instead, the images are small, hemmed in by UI chrome, and buried below the fold on most devices. The food is supposed to be the star here.

04 · The Redesign

Domino's Pizza — Redesigned

Five changes. No new features, no restructured flows, no new information architecture. Just fixing what's already broken and getting out of the food's way.

1
Move the Login Gate to the ADD tap

Let users browse freely. Login only triggers when they actually try to add something — standard e-commerce behaviour. Guest browsing first, account friction only when it's unavoidable.

2
Lead with the Hero Image

The restaurant's food photograph takes full width at the top. Restaurant name and rating sit on top of it. The food is the first thing you see. The food is the point.

3
ADD Button in Zomato Red #E23744

White text, rounded corners, full confidence. Red means act now. The button finally looks like a purchase action instead of a spreadsheet input.

4
Demote the Warning Banner to a Footnote

The yellow delivery banner moves to the very bottom as small-print text. It's a legal disclosure, not a headline. Treat it accordingly.

5
Consolidate the Info Strip

Delivery time, distance, area, rating, and offer chip into one clean row below the hero. One scan, done. Hierarchy restored.

The redesign isn't trying to reinvent Zomato — it's trying to stop Zomato from undermining itself. The brand is strong. The discounts are real. The design just needs to get out of the way.

05 · What Zomato Gets Right

Credit Where It's Due

The restaurant page problems don't define the whole product. Zomato has some genuinely impressive stuff that doesn't get enough credit.

✓ Live Order Tracking
Best-in-class real-time map

The live delivery map is genuinely ahead of the industry. Nobody does this better right now.

✓ Rating System
Photo reviews + dish-level ratings

Verified purchase tags, photos with reviews, ratings tied to specific dishes. Actually useful, not just decorative.

✓ Dish-Level Search
"Paneer tikka near me"

Search by dish, not just restaurant. No competitor has cracked this as well. It changes how people discover food.

✓ Home Screen
Cleaner than people think

White background, large cards, restrained palette. The home is actually better than Swiggy's — which makes the restaurant page failures even more frustrating.

✓ Filter Chips
Veg / Non-Veg / Top rated / Bolt

Fast, functional, well-placed. Lets users narrow intent in one tap. Small thing, done right.

✓ Offer Chip Design
The pink pill works

The compact offer pill on listing cards is elegant. It's the yellow mid-screen banner on the restaurant page that's the problem — not offers themselves.

06 · Verdict

The Scorecard

Tallying it up across the six dimensions that matter most:

Dimension Zomato Swiggy
Home Screen Cleaner, more restrained Busier, more cluttered
Restaurant Page Critical UX failures Quieter, more confident
Order Tracking Best-in-class Good but secondary
Perceived Trust Desperate — over-promotes Confident, lets food lead
Search Depth Dish-level search Standard restaurant search
Overall Feel Capable, unpolished Premium, considered

Zomato isn't losing on price. It's losing on perception. And perception is something a design team can actually fix.

"Zomato doesn't have a price problem. It has a confidence problem. Fix the restaurant page and the price advantage starts to mean something."
