Case Study — Apple Music

From albums
to songs

A focused redesign of Apple Music's home screen to surface songs directly — removing the album and playlist layer that stands between the user and the music they want to hear.

01 — The problem

Apple Music hides songs behind albums

The most common action in any music app is playing a song. Apple Music's home screen makes you navigate through at least one container — an album or a playlist — before you can do that.

When you open Apple Music, the first thing you see is two oversized editorial cards taking up nearly the entire screen. Below them, a "Recently Played" row that shows album covers and playlist thumbnails. Further down, Apple's own curated sections dominate the scroll. At no point on the home screen can you tap directly on a song and hear it.

This is not a broken product — Apple Music works fine and millions of people use it every day. But the home screen is organised around content containers rather than content itself. The hierarchy serves Apple's editorial agenda before it serves the listener's actual behaviour. People think in songs. The home screen thinks in albums.

The redesign question was simple: what would the home screen look like if it was designed around how people actually listen, rather than how Apple wants them to discover?

Problem statement
"Apple Music's home screen organises content around albums and playlists, forcing users to navigate through containers to reach the songs they actually want to play — adding unnecessary friction to the most common action in the app."
What the original screen looks like

The original Apple Music home screen annotated — four hierarchy problems identified across the full scroll

Problem 1
Two cards fill the entire first view. Only two items are visible above the fold. Both are large editorial cards. Users have to scroll before seeing anything based on their own listening history.
Problem 2
Recently Played shows albums, not songs. Every item in the recently played row is a container. Tapping Winning Speech takes you to the album, not to the song you were actually listening to.
Problem 3
No song-level row exists anywhere on the screen. There is no section that surfaces individual songs directly. All discovery routes through albums or playlists first, adding a minimum of one extra tap to every action.
Problem 4
Editorial content outweighs personal listening. Apple's curated sections like Punjabi Pop and Hindi Pop Throwback take up more vertical space than the user's own recently played content. The priorities are inverted.
02 — Research

Real users, real frustration

Before designing anything, I spent time in App Store reviews and Reddit threads to understand whether what I was experiencing was a personal frustration or a broader pattern. It was both.

The App Store reviews were the most direct source. Filtering to one and two star reviews and reading through them revealed a clear pattern — the complaints were not about individual features being broken, they were about the overall experience feeling cluttered and hard to navigate. People were not comparing Apple Music to a broken product. They were comparing it to Spotify.

The Spotify comparison came up repeatedly, and it pointed directly at the home screen problem. Spotify's home screen greets you with a compact grid of recently played items — you see eight things in the space Apple uses for two. Everything is immediately tappable. There are no oversized editorial cards standing between you and your music on load.

On Reddit, the complaints were more nuanced. Users on r/ios discussed how search behaviour had changed between updates and how inconsistency across the app made muscle memory unreliable. One thread documented the frustration of not being able to find local library songs easily when the Apple Music subscription UI kept pushing itself forward. These are all symptoms of the same underlying problem — the app is organised around Apple's priorities, not the user's.

What Spotify gets right

I used Spotify as a reference point — not to copy it, but to understand what song-first discovery actually looks like when it is designed intentionally. Spotify's home screen opens with a 2x4 compact grid of recently played items. No oversized editorial cards. No albums you have to open before hearing anything. Eight items visible above the fold, all directly tappable.

The filter chips at the top of Spotify's home — All, Music, Podcasts — let users narrow what they see without navigating anywhere. This is the kind of control that makes a home screen feel personal rather than generic.

The goal was not to make Apple Music look like Spotify. The goal was to take what Spotify gets right about content density and song-first access, and apply it within Apple Music's own design language.

03 — Design process

How the redesign was built

The approach was constraint-first. This is Apple Music — the design language, the colour system, the typography, and the component patterns are already defined. The job was to reorganise the hierarchy, not invent a new visual system.

I set the design system before touching the canvas. SF Pro Display and SF Pro Text for typography, sourced from Apple's developer site and exported through the SF Symbols app. The full Apple Music dark mode colour palette — #000000 background, #1C1C1E card surface, #FA2D48 red accent, #8E8E93 secondary text. Everything native. The redesign needed to feel like it could ship from Apple, not like a student project with a custom aesthetic applied on top.

The screen was built zone by zone, with each zone reviewed before the next was started. This is the same process I used on Markd — define the structure, validate the layout, build, review, fix, move on. No zone was considered complete until it was correct.

AI was used as a thinking partner during this project — for stress-testing layout decisions, generating wireframe references, and flagging issues before they got embedded in the design. The visual work, the judgement calls, and the iteration were all done in Figma.

Structural decisions made before opening Figma
Decision What I chose Why
First element above the fold Resume card — last played song with one-tap play The most common returning user behaviour is resuming what they were last listening to. Surfacing this immediately above the fold removes the most frequent source of friction on the home screen.
Recently Played format Compact 2x4 grid with small thumbnails and titles The original horizontal scroll with large cards showed two items at once. The 2x4 grid shows eight in the same vertical space, making the section genuinely useful for scanning rather than just scrolling.
New section added Your Top Songs — individual song rows with play buttons No version of the original home screen surfaces individual songs directly. This section addresses the core problem statement by giving users a route to their most played tracks without navigating through a container first.
Filter chips Horizontally scrollable row: All, Songs, Albums, Playlists, Radio Filter chips give users immediate control over what content type they see on the home screen. The "Songs" filter in particular makes the song-first intent of the redesign explicit and actionable.
Curated sections Kept but reduced — smaller cards, positioned after personal content Removing Apple's curated sections entirely would have been an overreach. The redesign keeps them as secondary content, positioned below the user's own listening data rather than above it.
Ideas considered and rejected
Two-tab layout — "Songs" and "Albums" tabs inside the home screen
Rejected
What was considered
Splitting the home screen into two tabs — one showing song-level content, one showing albums and editorial sections — so users could switch between modes depending on what they wanted.
Apple Music already has tab-based navigation at the bottom of the screen: Home, New, Radio, Library. Adding another tab layer inside the Home screen would create nested navigation — tabs within tabs — which adds cognitive complexity rather than reducing it. The user would need to learn two navigation systems simultaneously. The filter chip approach achieves the same goal without the hierarchy problem. Chips filter the existing single screen; they do not create new destinations.
Removing the curated editorial sections entirely
Rejected
What was considered
Stripping out Punjabi Pop, Hindi Pop Throwback, and the other Apple editorial playlist sections from the home screen entirely, replacing them with more personal content.
This would have been an overreach. The problem is not that curated sections exist — it is that they are prioritised over personal content. Apple's editorial curation is genuinely useful for discovery. The right fix is to move it below the fold, not to remove it. Keeping curated sections as the last zone on the screen gives them an appropriate role: they are something to explore when you are done with what you came for, not the first thing you see when you open the app.
Full app redesign — not just the home screen
Out of scope
What was considered
Extending the redesign beyond the home screen to include the Library, Search, and Now Playing screens for a more complete product vision.
Breadth without depth is the most common failure mode in redesign case studies. A full-app redesign that touches everything shallowly is less impressive than a focused redesign that solves one problem with real rigour. The home screen is the entry point for every session — fixing it well is a complete and defensible scope. The Now Playing screen, the Library, and Search all have their own problems worth solving, but each deserves its own focused case study rather than a shallow pass as part of a broader effort.
Iteration log

Resume card. The first version had the song title at too large a size — reading at around 20px, which made it compete visually with the "Home" page title above it. Reduced to 14px Medium. The "CONTINUE PLAYING" label above it was also missing in the initial build, which meant the card had no context — a user seeing it for the first time would not immediately understand what it was for. Adding the uppercase label fixed this.

Filter chips. The "All" chip was flush against the left edge of the frame with no margin in the first version. Moving the entire chip row to start at 20px from the left edge brought it into alignment with the rest of the content. The chips themselves were also too large initially — reduced to 6px vertical padding and 14px horizontal padding to bring them closer to the compact chip style used across iOS.

Recently Played grid. The album art squares had a corner radius that was too large in the first version, making them look like app icons rather than music thumbnails. Reducing the corner radius to 4px brought them back in line with how Apple Music renders album art elsewhere in the product.

Download Report button colour. This was on the Report screen in the Markd project — but the same lesson applied here. When two elements share the same visual weight and colour, neither reads as more important. Every element on the redesigned screen was checked against this principle: one primary accent colour, used only where it earns its place.

04 — The solution

The redesigned home screen

The redesign keeps everything Apple Music already does — the dark aesthetic, the navigation, the editorial sections — and reorganises the hierarchy so that the user's own listening comes first.

The redesigned Apple Music home screen — same design language, reorganised hierarchy

What changed and why
Resume card
The last played song sits at the top with a one-tap play button. This directly addresses the most common returning user behaviour — picking up where you left off — without requiring any navigation. The original screen had no equivalent of this.
Compact 2x4 grid
Recently Played now shows eight items in the space the original used for two. Each item is directly tappable. The grid format makes scanning fast — your eye moves across eight thumbnails in a second rather than scrolling through a horizontal row of oversized cards.
Your Top Songs
A section that did not exist in the original. Three song rows with album art, song name, artist, and a play button. For the first time on the Apple Music home screen, a user can hear a specific song with one tap without opening anything first.
Filter chips
All, Songs, Albums, Playlists, Radio — horizontally scrollable. Selecting Songs filters the home screen to show only song-level content. This makes the intent of the redesign actionable and gives users explicit control over their home screen experience.
The goal was never to make Apple Music look different. It was to make it feel like it knows you — like it is organised around what you actually came to do, not around what Apple wants to promote.
05 — Reflection

What I learned

Redesigning an existing product is a different skill from designing something new. You are working within constraints you did not set, and the bar for justifying every change is higher because there is an existing baseline to compare against.

The most important discipline in this project was restraint. It would have been easy to redesign the entire app — the Now Playing screen, the Library, the Search experience — and present a sweeping vision. But a case study that touches everything shallowly is less valuable than one that solves one problem with real depth. Keeping the scope to the home screen forced every decision to be justified against a specific problem rather than a general desire to make things look better.

The research phase changed the direction of the project. I went in thinking the problem was playlist creation — that was my original complaint. The App Store reviews and my own experience using the app pointed me toward the home screen hierarchy instead. Following the research rather than the initial assumption led to a stronger, more defensible problem statement.

Working within Apple's existing design language was both a constraint and an advantage. It removed a lot of decisions — I did not have to choose colours, fonts, or component styles. But it also raised the standard for everything I did design, because any deviation from Apple's patterns would read as inconsistency rather than creativity. Every element in the redesign had to feel like it belonged to the same product.

If I were to take this further, the Songs filter chip state would be the next thing to design — showing what the home screen looks like when only song-level content is visible. The Now Playing screen is also a candidate for a separate focused redesign. And there is an open question about what happens to the Recently Played grid when the user has more than eight items — a "See all" state that expands the grid into a full list view would complete the interaction.
