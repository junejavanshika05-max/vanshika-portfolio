01 Problem & Context

EduPath wasn't failing at teaching.
It was failing before teaching began.

New users signed up with energy and intention — then landed on a catalogue of 400+ courses with no personalisation, no recommended starting point, and no visible sense of where to begin.

The result was stark. 68% of new users left within 7 days and never came back. The product was offering a library when users needed a guide.

"What a new user needs in the first 60 seconds is not more content. It is a sense of being understood."

— Core Design Insight

😤 Excited but overwhelmed

They signed up with energy and intention, but 400+ courses with no direction immediately drained that motivation. Excitement turned to paralysis.

🤔 Uncertain and self-doubting

They had a vague goal but didn't know where they sat on the skill spectrum. The catalogue offered no mirror.

🎯 Purposeful but impatient

They had a specific outcome in mind and needed the app to understand that quickly. Any friction felt like a barrier.

💡 The opportunity

Most competitors were either bright and gamified or cold and corporate. A warm, guided, mentor-led experience occupied a genuine gap.

Sign Up

Leo appears before the student even creates an account — establishing from the very first screen that EduPath is a guided experience with a presence, not just another sign-up form.

Two-column layout: Leo on the left, clean white card form on the right. This immediately differentiates EduPath from every generic onboarding in the market.

Design Decision

Leading with Leo before the form creates an emotional connection before any data is collected. The student meets their mentor before they commit.

Screen 03

The Card Table

Rather than a sequential form, students see 5 face-down mystery cards in a gentle arc. The centre card is raised and glowing — but the student can tap any card in any order they choose.

Dark olive cards with golden yellow borders. The centre card has a sage green glow to attract attention without forcing a choice.

Design Decision

A standard question form risks feeling like a chore. By turning each question into a card reveal, the student is actively discovering rather than passively answering. Freedom reinforces autonomy from the very first interaction.

Screen 04

Card Flip & Question

When a card is tapped it expands in place. The 4 remaining cards shrink to 60% opacity — still visible so the student stays oriented. The question is revealed with 4 styled answer options.

Selected options fill terracotta. The Next button stays disabled until an answer is selected — ensuring complete data for personalisation without feeling punishing.

Design Decision

Disabling Next until an answer is chosen ensures complete personalisation data without a wall of required field errors. The student simply can't skip — but it never feels like a punishment.

Screen 05

The Tarot Reveal

Once all 5 questions are answered, the screen shifts dramatically — dark olive background with a warm radial golden glow. Three reveal cards appear: Foundations, Your Focus (hero, gold-bordered), and Mastery (locked, dashed).

Leo is deliberately absent here. The cards take centre stage for a moment of genuine discovery. His presence is felt through the copy.

Design Decision

The "Start here" button was removed from Card 1 — the student must confirm the full path first. Two CTAs: confirm in terracotta (primary) or customise as outline (secondary).

Screen 07

Home Screen

Leo anchors the left column — full body, thumbs up, welcoming the student back. All learning content lives in the right column with a clear hierarchy.

The Resume card is the unmissable hero action. Tabs separate active courses from recommendations. The streak badge sits quietly in the greeting row — present but never competing.

Design Decision

The path roadmap was removed from the home screen entirely — it lives in the "My Path" nav destination. Home is purely about learning activity, not navigation. No redundancy.

07 Reflection
"EduPath's problem was never the content. It was the distance between a student's intention and their first meaningful action."

The onboarding gives students a voice before the app speaks to them. The home screen gives them a path, not just a library. The retention system gives them reasons to come back that go beyond habit.

This case study was also a personal one. As a student who has felt lost and overwhelmed by the very apps meant to help me learn — I designed EduPath to be the thing I always wished existed. Leo is the mentor I never had. The card mechanic is the experience that would have made me stay.

The biggest lesson: good UX is not about features. It is about the feeling a product gives you in the first 60 seconds.
