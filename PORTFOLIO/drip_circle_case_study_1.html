Overview

The brief and the team

Drip is a fictional global fast fashion retailer. The brief: design a new service that supports a sustainability pilot, keeps garments in circulation, and complements Drip's existing business rather than replacing it.

👥
Team: Vanshika Juneja · René Scheuch · Tingting Shi · Clara Pei-yu Wang · Silvia Zverbikova  |  Module: IS6147 Design Thinking for Services  |  Programme: DDDB

Our proposed solution is DRIP Circle — a service that combines an in-store garment return and rewards system with a digital app for engagement, discovery, and social sharing. Customers return unwanted garments for points, browse a curated selection of pre-loved pieces, get styling inspiration, and track the environmental impact of their choices.

The service was designed with a clear principle at its core: sustainable behaviour must feel rewarding before it can feel virtuous. Every decision — from how condition tiers are named to how the campaign banner is placed — reflects this. We designed for real people operating in a system built against them, not for an idealised version of an eco-conscious consumer who doesn't exist.

01 — Research

Understanding why sustainable fashion fails

Before generating any solutions, the team spent significant time understanding the real shape of the problem — through stakeholder mapping, 15 semi-structured interviews, secondary research, and a safari of fast fashion stores in Cork city.

Primary research

15 interviews. One consistent pattern.

Interviews were conducted with students aged 19 to 23 across Cork, Dublin, Galway, Limerick and Wicklow. Four themes came through consistently across almost every conversation.

Finding 01
Distrust of sustainability claims. Almost every participant described being sceptical of the word "sustainable" being used by brands. This wasn't scepticism about sustainability as a concept — it was distrust directed specifically at brands, built up over years of vague claims with no way to verify them.
Finding 02
Price as a structural barrier. For budget-constrained participants, sustainable fashion simply wasn't a choice — it was unaffordable. For more financially comfortable participants, price still mattered because sustainable options didn't feel worth the premium when claims couldn't be verified.
Finding 03
The attitude-behaviour gap in action. Multiple participants were articulate about the problems with fast fashion and still bought fast fashion regularly. The guilt was real and present, but it didn't change behaviour. Adding more information or guilt-inducing messaging would not help.
Finding 04
Convenience is the real competitor. Fast fashion wins not just because it's cheap, but because it's easy — apps designed to maximise impulse buying, next-day delivery, endless new drops. Several participants described not having the time or energy to research ethical options even when they wanted to.
Secondary research

What the academic literature confirmed

The team's secondary research centred on Schiaroli, Fraccascia and Dangelico (2024), a systematic review of 217 academic papers on sustainable consumer behaviour in fashion, published in the Journal of Cleaner Production. The study analysed drivers and barriers across three phases — buying, using, and disposing of clothes.

The key finding was that environmental concern plays a secondary role in purchase decisions. Price, style, comfort and quality matter far more to most consumers. The main barriers to sustainable fashion are high cost, distrust of brand claims, the perception that sustainable clothing looks unfashionable, limited availability, and deeply ingrained shopping habits.

The core takeaway that shaped the entire design direction: sustainable fashion fails when it leads with guilt or information, and succeeds when it is stylish, affordable, easy, and rewarding. This directly informed why DRIP Circle was built around rewards and convenience rather than moral obligation.

"The barriers to sustainable fashion are structural, not attitudinal. Consumers are not indifferent — they are operating in a system that makes unsustainable choices easier at every stage."
— Schiaroli, Fraccascia & Dangelico (2024), Journal of Cleaner Production
Safari findings

In-store observation

The team visited H&M, Penneys, and Urban Outfitters in Cork. The most striking finding: none of the stores had a dedicated sustainable section. Sustainability was either invisible or passive — existing in small tags, optional QR codes, and background initiatives that required the customer to already be looking for it. Nothing was designed to meet a customer where they are and make sustainable choices feel like the obvious option.

Penneys was the most interesting — several garments had recycled material tags and there was a Primark Cotton Project with QR code posters. But the effort was passive. Urban Outfitters' mannequin styling — showing how to combine fewer pieces in different ways — was an unintentional sustainability insight: the industry already has tools for communicating versatility, it just isn't connecting them to sustainability.

02 — Personas

Four consumers. Four different barriers.

Four personas were developed from the interview clusters. Each represents a different relationship with fast fashion and sustainability — and together they cover the full range of financial situations, values, and behaviours the service needed to account for.

Aoife
The Functional Minimalist
Trust Kills Intent
"Everyone claims to be sustainable now because it sells. Doesn't mean much without evidence." She researches brands, reads labels, and compares options — and still often hits a dead end because sustainability claims are impossible to verify.
Ciarán
The Overburdened Sceptic
Trust Kills Intent
"We're not ignorant, we're conflicted. The system makes it really easy to do the wrong thing and really hard to do the right thing." He holds brands to a high standard but greenwashing makes him increasingly cynical.
Sharon
The Working Mother
Budget Kills Intent
"The sustainable fashion movement needs to centre accessibility and affordability, or it's just another form of classism dressed up as ethics." For Sharon, the cheapest option isn't a preference — it's the only one available.
Carlin
The Trend-Trapped Creative
Style Kills Intent
"The guilt is real but so is the want." Fashion is identity for Carlin — she needs sustainable options to meet her where she is aesthetically, not judge her from a moral high ground she can't afford to reach.
The four personas kept the team honest throughout the ideation process. Any concept that only worked for eco-conscious consumers who already had options failed the Sharon test. Any concept that wasn't visually exciting failed the Carlin test.
03 — Ideation

From research to direction

The team used How Might We framing, SCAMPER, and concept clustering to move from problem understanding to solution generation. Ideas were generated on Miro before being cleaned, grouped, and voted on.

SCAMPER method

Seven angles. One consistent direction.

S
Substitute
Replace guilt-driven sustainability messaging with credit points earned on every return. Substitute cheap disposable garments with durable quality materials. Replace fast fashion messaging with slow fashion values and styling skills.
C
Combine
Combine the online shopping checkout with a built-in garment return scheme. Combine loyalty rewards with sustainable actions like returning or repairing. Host free repair workshops in Drip stores that merge skill-building, social experience and garment extension into one touchpoint.
A
Adapt
Adapt the airline loyalty model — reward customers with CO₂ savings they can spend in-store. Adapt the Netflix subscription model — pay monthly to access a rotating Drip wardrobe. Adapt the bottle deposit model — add a refundable deposit redeemed only on return.
M
Modify
Modify language from guilt to motivation — frame sustainable behaviour as something that earns rather than costs. Modify the social aspect of secondhand, making resale a hype-driven shareable moment like a new drop. Slow down the promotion cycle to reduce impulse buying.
P
Put to other use
Use underused Drip stores to host repair and upcycling workshops. Use consumer data not to sell more but to manufacture less. Use returned damaged garments as raw repair material for in-store repair services.
E
Eliminate
Eliminate guilt-driven sustainability language entirely from all Drip communications — the highest-voted idea across the Miro board. Eliminate complicated environmental information that overwhelms rather than motivates. Eliminate the research burden by making transparency automatic rather than effortful.
R
Reverse
Reverse ownership — shift from selling clothes to renting them. Reverse garment design — build end-of-life into the product from day one using modular components and mono-materials. Reverse the traditional make-sell-dispose chain to collect-remake-resell.
The chosen direction

Three top solutions

Solution 01
Replace guilt with rewards
Make returning a Drip garment as rewarding as buying something new. Points earned upfront, before the user commits — so the value is clear from the start.
Solution 02
Embed circularity in the platform
Make Drip's circular service as frictionless as adding something to a Shein cart. Return, browse secondhand, and track impact — all within one existing platform.
Solution 03
Repurpose stores as hubs
Transform Drip's physical stores from sales spaces into circular fashion community hubs — where repair, upcycling and styling become social experiences people want to attend.
Problem statement
"Consumers who want to engage in more sustainable and creative approaches to fashion are unable to do so consistently due to the demands of everyday life, financial constraints, and the constant pressure of social media trend cycles that make fast fashion the path of least resistance."

Key design decisions

Every decision justified by research

1
Navy blue as primary action colour — not green
Despite DRIP Circle being a sustainability-focused service, navy blue was chosen as the primary action colour. The research found that participants consistently associated green with greenwashing and described sustainability branding as performative. Navy signals trustworthiness and premium quality without triggering the scepticism that green-heavy branding provokes. Green was retained but used sparingly — on certification badges, the Pre-loved tag, and the Scan Tag screen — so that when it appears, it carries specific meaning rather than functioning as decoration.
2
Re-DRIP tab name — not Return or Recycle
The return tab was named Re-DRIP because "recycle" tested poorly in interviews, feeling obligatory and guilt-driven. Naming it within the DRIP brand language keeps the action feeling like part of the lifestyle rather than a separate environmental duty. This reflects the broader insight that moral messaging backfires with this audience, and that sustainable behaviour is more likely when it feels natural and rewarding rather than dutiful.
3
Points shown upfront before commitment
Points are placed at the very top of the home screen and shown upfront on the condition check screen because the research identified reward as the primary driver of behaviour change. If the incentive is buried or revealed only after the user commits to the return, the motivation to act is weakened. Showing the value immediately before the user has to do anything removes the uncertainty that causes drop-off.
4
Campaign as inline card, not popup
The marketing campaign is embedded as an inline card on the home screen rather than a popup because participants described intrusive promotional messaging as a reason they disengage from brand apps. An inline card respects the user's attention while still surfacing promotional content, maintaining commercial functionality without creating friction. The user can engage with it or scroll past — it never interrupts.
5
Condition tiers: Like New, Good, Well-loved
The three condition tiers use human, non-judgmental language rather than technical grades. "Well-loved" in particular reframes wear as character rather than damage, reducing the perceived barrier to returning items that aren't in perfect condition. If users feel their returned items will be unfairly assessed or dismissed, they won't engage with the return flow at all — a direct lesson from the Sharon persona.
6
Secondhand as a separate "Shop Re-Second Hand" tab
The marketplace was split into Shop New and Shop Re-Second Hand because conflating new and secondhand stock risks making pre-loved items feel like a lesser option. Giving secondhand its own dedicated space frames circular stock as a distinct and desirable category rather than a discounted fallback — directly addressing the persona of the budget-constrained consumer who is already engaging in sustainable behaviour but doesn't see it reflected in the products available.
7
Near You includes UCC Student Centre drop point
The Discovery Map feature includes a Community Drop Point at UCC Student Centre because the research was conducted entirely with Irish university students, and physical accessibility was identified as a practical barrier. A campus drop point removes the need for a dedicated trip to a high street store, reducing the effort cost of returning items for the most price-sensitive segment of the user base.
8
Choose Return Method screen retained as a dismissed idea
The screen presenting Free Mail Return, Drop at Drip Store, and Request Pickup was considered for removal during team review. The team decided to retain it in the prototype but document it as a dismissed idea — while it is a strong design concept, offering free mail return and doorstep pickup is not financially feasible in the Irish market at this time. For the current pilot scope, in-store drop-off is the primary return method.

Wireflows

Navigation logic

The wireflow maps how users navigate between the 11 screens, combining screen structure with directional flow logic. It distinguishes between primary navigation — always accessible via the bottom tab bar — and secondary navigation within individual flows such as the three-step return process.

The shop screen connects directly from the home screen via the bottom navigation tab rather than from the return success screen. This was deliberate — after completing a return the user lands back on the home screen with their updated points balance, and any decision to browse the shop comes from their own intent rather than being automatically pushed. Users should feel in control rather than directed.

The marketing campaign banner does not appear as a separate screen in the wireflow. It is embedded as an inline card on the home screen, always present without requiring its own navigation path. The user can tap it to reach the relevant shop section, or scroll past it without any disruption.

06 — Reflection

What we dismissed and what we learned

Not every idea that emerged from ideation made it into the final service. Documenting what was considered and why it was set aside is as important as documenting what was kept.

Dismissed — C2C Marketplace (Consumer to Consumer)
A consumer-to-consumer marketplace function, rental, and swap features were considered and discarded. After evaluating buyer-paid fees, seller service realities, and shipping partnerships, the team prioritised DRIP's own curated secondhand selection rather than complicating the app's functionality. A C2C model would have added significant operational complexity for a pilot service and risked diluting the core return-and-reward proposition.
Dismissed — Self-service kiosks for returning
The team originally considered self-service kiosks at return points. After looking closely at the staff inspection procedure, it became clear that garment conditions are too varied to be assessed reliably by machines. Manual classification was necessary to ensure fair and transparent rewards. The QR code scanning feature from the original kiosk concept was retained — customers can scan a garment tag in-app and generate a QR code before visiting the store to accelerate the intake process.
Deferred — Garment traceability after return
Showing customers the exact journey of their returned garment — where it went, who bought it — was initially included as a concept. While it would give users a strong sense of involvement, the practicality of garment-level tracing was identified as out of scope for the pilot. It was moved to Future Extensions and could be implemented in a later phase, potentially using EU Digital Product Passport technology.
Deferred — Workshops and designer collaborations
Workshops for repair and upcycling, and collaborations with local designers for the "Reborn Selection" were both strong concepts but treated as future extensions rather than pilot requirements. The focused MVP — return clothes, get fair rewards, best items resold in a curated section — needed to work before adding the complexity of physical programming and external partnerships.
Potential risks

What could go wrong

Risk
In-store storage overload
If garment returns exceed processing capacity, the system breaks down. The service needs to dynamically adjust incentives based on capacity while simultaneously increasing garment outflow through resale, refurbishment, and recycling channels.
Risk
Inconsistent grading
If garment assessment is inconsistent, trust in the reward system collapses. Standardised grading criteria, staff training, and visible guidelines are all needed to ensure the same garment receives the same reward regardless of which counter or staff member handles it.
Risk
Long counter wait times
Poor in-store experience at the return counter would immediately undermine the service's promise of convenience. Staff allocation aligned to store traffic patterns, and encouragement of QR pre-processing via the app, would reduce in-store handling time significantly.
Risk
Point value confusion
Abstract points systems fail when users can't translate them into real value. The solution is real-world equivalents shown clearly — "100 points = €1" — throughout the app, so the reward always feels tangible and worth the return effort.
Drip's customers are not ignorant. They are conflicted. A service that removes barriers will always outperform one that adds guilt.
